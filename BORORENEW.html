The Borosil Story — Why the numbers look messy (and why that may matter for 5-year investors)
FY20–FY22: Profitable years. India's solar sector growing. Company making money as domestic supplier.
FY23–FY25: China floods India with cheap solar glass, undercutting domestic prices. Borosil posts losses for 3 consecutive years. EBITDA margin crashes from ~15% to 3.9% in FY25.
Jul 2025: German subsidiary GMB Glasmanufaktur (Europe's largest solar glass maker) files for insolvency. Borosil writes off entire German exposure (~₹360 Cr exceptional charge over 9M FY26).
Dec 2024 / May 2025: GAME CHANGER — India imposes 5-year anti-dumping duty on Chinese and Vietnamese solar glass (up to USD 664/tonne). Chinese competition neutralised.
Q3 FY26 (Dec 2025): Record quarterly profit — ₹100 Cr PAT, Revenue ₹390 Cr (+40% YoY India standalone). EBITDA margin 31.5%.
May 2025: Board approves 600 TPD capacity expansion (₹950 Cr). Two 300 TPD furnaces. Target: Oct–Dec 2026 commissioning. India-only focus going forward.
⚠ GERMAN SUBSIDIARY WRITE-OFF (₹360 Cr EXCEPTIONAL LOSS)
Borosil had invested in GMB Glasmanufaktur Brandenburg GmbH and Geosphere Glassworks GmbH in Germany. Both went insolvent (GMB: Jul 2025, Geosphere: Dec 2025). Full provision of ₹360 Cr taken in FY26. This was a significant capital allocation mistake by management and has clouded consolidated FY26 numbers. The silver lining: the exposure is now fully written off — no further losses expected from Germany.
⚠ PROMOTER STAKE DECLINING (−343 BPS IN 3 QUARTERS)
Promoter holding has declined from ~62% to 58.8% over the last 3 quarters (Sep 2025 to Dec 2025 period). While still high at 58.8%, the downward trend warrants monitoring. Promoter pledging remains 0% — no distress signal, but gradual stake reduction is worth watching.

The valuation context — why forward matters more than trailing
Trailing numbers are misleading: FY23–FY25 losses (Chinese dumping) + ₹360 Cr German write-off in FY26 = all historical metrics look terrible (negative ROE, distorted P/E, eroded book value).

What Q3 FY26 proves: India operations can generate ₹100 Cr profit/quarter (₹400 Cr annualised) with current capacity. EBITDA margin of 31.5% is world-class for a specialty glass manufacturer — better than what Chinese competitors were making before dumping.

The forward case: Anti-dumping duty runs until ~Dec 2029. ₹950 Cr capacity expansion doubles output by end of 2026. If these execute, FY28–29 could deliver ₹600–800 Cr PAT — at current market cap of ₹5,591 Cr, that's a P/E of ~7–9x. For a monopoly with protected margins, that's potentially very cheap.

The risk: The market knows all this — the stock ran to ₹721 (before Q1 FY26 German write-off hit). The current ₹415 CMP reflects execution risk (₹950 Cr capex), AD duty renewal risk, and distrust from the German misadventure.
Overall valuation: MIXED — CHEAP ON FORWARD, EXPENSIVE ON TRAILING
Trailing metrics are not the right lens — this is a pure turnaround. Forward P/E of ~14x on FY27E earnings looks attractive for India's only solar glass manufacturer. However, P/B of 6.5x and execution risks (₹950 Cr capex, AD duty reliance) mean this is not a straightforward "cheap" call. It's a bet on structural earnings recovery materialising.

MODERATE FUNDAMENTALS — HIGH-STAKES TURNAROUND WITH MONOPOLY UPSIDE
Borosil Renewables is India's only solar glass manufacturer, emerging from 3 years of China-induced losses after a 5-year government anti-dumping duty has transformed operations — Q3 FY26 delivered record ₹100 Cr quarterly PAT and 31.5% EBITDA margins. The 5-year investment thesis is compelling, but hinges critically on: (1) anti-dumping duty renewal in 2029, and (2) ₹950 Cr capacity expansion executing on time.
What works for this stock
✓Domestic monopoly, government-protected: India's ONLY large-scale solar glass manufacturer, shielded by a 5-year anti-dumping duty on Chinese/Vietnamese glass (Dec 2024–Dec 2029). EBITDA margins jumped from 3.9% (FY25) to 31.5% (Q3 FY26) in two quarters — this is not a recovery, this is a structural reset.
✓India's solar ambition is the demand engine: India targets 500 GW renewable energy by 2030 (currently ~180 GW). Each GW of solar requires ~5–6 million sq. metres of solar glass. Borosil's expanding capacity (6.5 GW → 11 GW) is positioned directly in the path of this structural demand wave.
✓Low debt + strong promoter backing: D/E of just 0.2x with zero promoter pledging means the company can fund the ₹950 Cr expansion without distress. The Borosil Group (58.8% promoter) has been committed through even the worst loss years — confidence signal.
What to watch
⚠Anti-dumping duty expiry in Dec 2029 — the existential risk: The entire turnaround is predicated on AD duty protection. If the duty expires in 2029 and isn't renewed, Chinese competition returns and margins could collapse back to 3–5%. For a 5-year investor, the duty renewal decision (likely 2028–2029) is the single most important event to monitor.
⚠₹950 Cr capacity expansion — execution and demand risk: If the two new 300 TPD furnaces face cost overruns, delays, or commissioning issues, or if India's solar addition pace slows, the expansion capital could sit underutilised. Management's German adventure showed they can make poor capital allocation calls internationally — though the India expansion is on home turf and policy-supported.
Track this going forward
→Q4 FY26 + FY27 EBITDA margins and capacity utilisation: Watch every quarter whether the 28–32% EBITDA margin is holding — this is the proof of concept for the AD duty impact. Also track: (1) ₹950 Cr capex progress and commissioning dates; (2) Any government signals on AD duty renewal post-2029; (3) Whether promoter stake trimming continues or stabilises.

Definitions — tap to expand

Anti-dumping duty — A protective tariff imposed when a foreign country sells goods below fair market value. India imposed USD 664/tonne on Chinese/Vietnamese solar glass to prevent them from undercutting domestic prices. Effective Dec 2024, valid for 5 years.

TPD — Tonnes per day. A measure of glass furnace capacity. 600 TPD expansion = two new furnaces of 300 TPD each, adding ~4.5 GW of solar glass output.

EBITDA margin — Operating profit as % of revenue. 31.5% EBITDA margin (Q3 FY26) means ₹31.5 of every ₹100 of revenue becomes operating profit. World-class for a materials manufacturer.

EV/EBITDA — Full enterprise value (market cap + net debt) divided by operating profit. Lower = better value. Useful when P/E is distorted by losses.

Exceptional items — One-time charges not part of regular operations. The ₹360 Cr German write-off is an exceptional item — it distorts FY26 headline PAT but doesn't reflect ongoing India business performance.

P/B ratio — Price vs book value (net assets). High P/B (6.5x) partly reflects years of losses eroding the equity base. As profitability recovers and book value builds, P/B will normalise.

Interest coverage — EBIT divided by interest expense. Negative in FY25 (EBIT was negative). Now recovering with profitable Q3 FY26 — will normalise to healthy levels in FY27.

CAGR — Compound Annual Growth Rate. The average yearly growth rate over a period.
